# Pop Goes the Weasel

## Origin
English nursery rhyme and dance dating to the 1850s. Originally a popular dance tune in Victorian England. "Pop" likely means to pawn, and "weasel" may be Cockney rhyming slang for "weasel and stoat" = coat. So the verse may describe pawning one's coat to buy thread.

## Musical Details
- **Key:** C major
- **Time Signature:** 6/8
- **Tempo:** ~120 BPM
- **Range:** C4–C5 (octave)
- **Difficulty:** Intermediate

## Lyrics
All around the mulberry bush,
The monkey chased the weasel,
The monkey thought 'twas all in fun,
Pop! Goes the weasel.

A penny for a spool of thread,
A penny for a needle,
That's the way the money goes,
Pop! Goes the weasel.

## Analysis
The 6/8 jig rhythm gives it a lively, dance-like character. The "Pop!" on the sudden leap is the signature moment — designed to surprise children. The A section and B section use different harmonic territory (C major vs F major area), creating variety. The repeating eighth-note patterns make it feel perpetual and bouncy.
